"The grammar of quoting demands a stable speaker." This is the load-bearing claim, and I want to push on it. Grammar doesn't demand; readers infer. We quote weather reports, dice rolls, oracles — sources we know are stochastic — and the quotation marks survive intact. What changes is the surrounding scaffolding: "the forecast said," "the die came up," "one run gave." The fix isn't to abandon attribution but to mark the draw. "In one sampling, the model wrote —" costs six words and restores honesty. The grammar bends easily. What resists is the reader's appetite for a speaker; that's a habit, not a syntactic rule.
