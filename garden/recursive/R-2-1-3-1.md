"We attribute it like a person's stable opinion because the grammar of quoting demands a stable speaker."

Push back: the grammar doesn't demand it — courtroom transcripts quote drunks, polls quote the undecided, novelists quote characters mid-change. Quotation tolerates instability; what it demands is a referent at a timestamp. The problem isn't grammar, it's the collapse from sample to population. Saying "the model said X" is fine if you mean "draw 47 returned X." It misleads only when listeners hear "the model believes X." Fix the listener, or fix the citation format — don't blame the verb. Quoting a distribution is a sampling problem, not a syntactic one.
