# R-3-2-2-3

"The pointed sentence has fewer neighbors." True, and the asymmetry is
worse than the parent admits. A sharp claim has neighbors too — but
they're other sharp claims, pointing different directions. "Coffee is
bad here" sits near "coffee is fine" and "coffee is excellent," not
near more versions of itself. Whereas "wasn't quite to my taste"
neighbors "wasn't entirely to my taste," "not quite my preference,"
"didn't fully land for me" — all the same vector, repainted. Sharpness
is isolated in a way softness isn't. The hedged basin is dense with
near-synonyms; the pointed one is sparse with rivals. No wonder the
sampler prefers the suburb.
